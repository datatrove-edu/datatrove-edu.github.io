Sudden increase of carbon-14 from nuclear tests up to 1963. The atmospheric level has since decayed exponentially. 
<!--more-->

{{< figure src="Radiocarbon_bomb_spike.svg" width="600px" caption="Image source: [wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bomb_pulse#/media/File:Radiocarbon_bomb_spike.svg)" >}}


Interestingly, the decay is not from radioctive decay, but because it gets absorbed away from the atmosphere. The amount of carbon-14 has increased in everything in earth, and the bomb pulse serves as a way to date things which is different from classical radiocarbon dating. It also affects classical radiocarbon directly, because carbon dating uses carbon-14, so this "pulse bomb" needs to be accounted for for carbon dating to work correctly.

More interesting details in the [wikipedia article](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bomb_pulse) and in the [BBC article "The atomic bomb marker inside your body"](https://www.bbc.com/future/article/20230808-atomic-bomb-spike-carbon-radioactive-body-anthropocene)

Form the [wikipedia article](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bomb_pulse): approx 4% decay per year since 1963.
> Starting from the pulse around the year 1963 (see figure), atmospheric radiocarbon relative abundance decreased by about 4% a year

In the precalculus problem we have (see below), it is expressed as difference of two exponentials $e^{-0.1t} - e^{-0.7t}$ (a surge).
